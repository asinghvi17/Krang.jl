Newmann-Penrose Formalism

The following is a summary of the Newmann-Penrose formalism. You can find a paper with more details below. [NP] The Newmann-Penrose (NP) Formalism is a set of notation that is ideal for expressing spacetime quantities in terms of a null-tetrad/null-spinoral basis.

Notation

We defined the $[T]_{x}$ notation which we will take to mean the tensor $T$ whose comonents are expressed with respect to a basis $x$.

Tetrad Calculus

The tetrad (vectoral basis) is defined by its metric components

\[g^{\mu\nu} =-l^\mu n^\nu-n^\mu l^\nu +m^\mu\bar m^\nu +\bar m^\mu m^\nu,\]

where the $l^\mu$ are typically taken to be the principle null direction of the Weyl tensor. It is typicall to express quantities in a tetradic basis such that the metric reduces is mapped to the double null Minkowski Metric. The tetrad basis is typically taken such that it indexes the tetrads, $l,n,m$ and $\bar m$ themselves. That is, one defines a change of basis

\[z_m{}^{\mu}=(l^\mu, n^\mu, m^\mu, \bar m^\mu)_m,\]

such that

\[[g^{\mu\nu}]_{GB}\rightarrow[\eta^{mn}]_{TB},\]

where $GB$ is short hand for 'Global Basis' and $TB$ for 'Tetrad Basis'.

Spinoral Calculus

It is possible to embed spinors into the vectorial representation of the various spacetime tensors. The canonical choice is to pick spinors, $\sigma^{\mu}{}_{AB'}$, which we embed in the metric in the following way:

\[g^{\mu\nu}\sigma^{\mu}_{AB'}\sigma^{\nu}_{CD'}=-\epsilon_{AC}\epsilon_{B'D'}\]

with $\epsilon$ representing the Levi-Civita symbols. The spinors act on a 2D complex vector space known as dyads. The dyads are typically indexed by the spinor indices in the following way;

\[\mathcal\zeta_{a}{}^A=(o^A,\iota^A)_a\]

such that

\[[g^{\mu\nu}]_{GB}\rightarrow[\epsilon_{ac}\epsilon_{b'd'}]_{SB},\]

where $SB$ is short hand for 'Spinoral Basis'. The chosen dyad basis satisfies the following relationships:

\[\sigma^A\iota^B\epsilon_{AB} = -\sigma^B\iota^A\epsilon_{AB} = 1,\]

and

\[\sigma^A\sigma^B\epsilon_{AB} = \iota^A\iota^B\epsilon_{AB} = 0.\]

The $\epsilon$ can be thought of as acting as the metric on dyads, taking the role of raising and lowering indices. This choice results in the $[\zeta_{aB}]_{SB}$ having the same indices as $\epsilon_{AB}$ itself.

An additional benefit from this choice of basis is that it defines an embedding of the spinoral basis in the tetrad basis in the following way:

\[\begin{align} l^\mu &=\sigma^\mu{}_{AB'}o^A\bar o^{B'}\\ n^\mu &=\sigma^\mu{}_{AB'}\iota^A\bar \iota^{B'}\\ m^\mu &=\sigma^\mu{}_{AB'}o^A\bar \iota^{B'}\\ \bar m^\mu &=\sigma^\mu{}_{AB'}\iota^A\bar o^{B'}. \end{align}\]

Ricci Rotation Coefficients

It is important to be able to define a connection and derivatives on tetrads and dyads. The covariant derivative on tetrads is adopted from its traditional definition on vectors. The covariant derivative on the tetrad basis is used to define the Ricci Rotation coefficients:

\[\gamma_m{}^{np}=z_{m\mu;\nu}z^{n\mu}z^{p\nu}\]

The covariant derivative on dyads however require specification. The covariant derivative on dyads are tradditionaly defined to annihilate $\epsilon_{AC}$, $\epsilon_{B'D'}$ and $\sigma^{\mu}{}_{AB'}$. The resulting covariant derivative on the dyad basis is then,

\[\zeta_{aB;\mu}=\Gamma_{aB\mu}=\zeta_{a}{}^{A}\Gamma_{AB\mu}=\zeta_{a}{}^{C}\epsilon_{CA}\epsilon^{AD}\Gamma_{DB\mu}=-\zeta_{aA}\Gamma^{A}{}_{B\mu}.\]

The dyadic equivalent of the Ricci rotation coefficients are then;

\[\Gamma_{abcd'}=\zeta_{aB;\mu}\zeta_{b}{}^B\sigma^{\mu}{}_{cd'}.\]
